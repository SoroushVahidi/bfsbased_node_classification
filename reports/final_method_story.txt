FINAL METHOD STORY — for PRL manuscript writing

PROBLEM
Standard graph-based node classifiers propagate neighbor information broadly.
On heterophilic graphs, where connected nodes often have different labels, this
propagation can be harmful — it systematically degrades predictions for nodes
whose neighborhoods are misleading.

WHAT FAILS IN PRIOR METHODS
The original selective graph correction (SGC v1) method trains an MLP, then
corrects uncertain nodes using weighted graph/feature evidence. While it
improves predictions on homophilic citation graphs on average, it has no
reliable way to withhold correction when graph evidence is untrustworthy.
In our frozen **10-split** benchmark, SGC v1 is worse than the MLP alone on
**Texas splits 4, 5, and 9** and on **Wisconsin split 8** (see
`reports/safety_analysis.md`).

WHAT FINAL V3 DOES DIFFERENTLY
We introduce a per-node graph reliability score R(v) that gates correction:

  R(v) = (neighbor_concentration + MLP_graph_agreement + degree_signal) / 3

Three interpretable signals, equally weighted:
  1. Neighbor concentration: do graph neighbors agree on a class?
  2. MLP-graph agreement: does the MLP prediction match the graph vote?
  3. Degree signal: does the node have enough neighbors for stable statistics?

Correction is applied ONLY when both conditions hold:
  - The MLP is uncertain (margin < τ)
  - The graph is reliable for this node (R(v) ≥ ρ)

Both τ and ρ are selected on validation data from a compact grid (~72 configs).

WHY IT WORKS
The reliability gate acts as a local quality check on neighborhoods:
  - On homophilic graphs, many nodes have high R(v) → corrections proceed where evidence is clear
  - On heterophilic graphs, many nodes have low R(v) → corrections are suppressed
This does not require a global homophily estimate — R(v) summarizes local neighborhood behavior.

WHERE IT WORKS BEST
  - Citation networks (Cora, Citeseer, Pubmed): clear gains over MLP with strong aggregate correction precision
  - Small heterophilic datasets (Texas, Wisconsin): conservative — reduces harmful-split count vs SGC v1 on this benchmark

LIMITATIONS
  - On strongly heterophilic Chameleon, mean gain vs MLP is tiny (+0.02 pp over 10 splits); split 4 shows a small negative Δ while means stay near the MLP
  - Texas split 5 is bad for SGC v1, V2, and FINAL_V3 alike in the frozen log — the gate does not magically fix every split
  - Post-hoc correction layer, not an end-to-end trained GNN; we do not claim to beat all GNNs on every benchmark

KEY NUMBERS (canonical **10-split** evaluation; see `tables/main_results_prl.md`)
  Mean Δ vs MLP across six datasets: **+1.26 pp** (FINAL_V3 vs MLP column means)
  Harmful splits (Δ test < 0 vs MLP): FINAL_V3 — **2 of 60**; SGC v1 — **4 of 60**
  Correction precision (aggregate on citations): high on Cora / Citeseer; see `reports/final_method_v3_analysis.md`
  Search space: ~72 configurations (2 weight profiles × ~9 τ × 4 ρ)
